12.2 Phishing Simulation Program

Phishing Campaign Best Practices

  • Gradual difficulty: Start easy, increase sophistication over time
  • Varied themes: IT alerts, HR notifications, package deliveries, current events
  • Immediate feedback: Educate users who click at the moment of failure
  • Positive reinforcement: Recognize users who report phishing attempts
  • No public shaming: Focus on improvement, not punishment
  • Regular cadence: Monthly campaigns to maintain vigilance

Interview Questions

12.3 Interview Questions & Answers

Q1 How would you build a security awareness program for a university?
Sample Answer

"A university presents unique challenges - diverse users, academic freedom culture, and limited time for training. My approach:

1. Audience Segmentation:

  • Students: Short, mobile-friendly content focused on personal security
  • Faculty: Research data protection, classroom device security
  • Staff: Compliance-focused, data handling procedures
  • IT: Advanced technical training, incident response

2. Delivery Methods:

  • Micro-learning modules (5-10 minutes)
  • Integration with LMS for tracking
  • Lunch-and-learn sessions
  • Security awareness month campaigns

3. Phishing Simulations:

  • Monthly campaigns with university-themed scenarios
  • Immediate training for those who click
  • Recognition for reporters

4. Metrics & Improvement:

  • Track click rates, report rates, training completion
  • Quarterly reports to leadership
  • Adjust content based on results
Q2 How do you handle users who repeatedly fail phishing tests?
Sample Answer

"Repeat clickers need additional support, not punishment:

Progressive Approach:

  • First failure: Immediate training module, reset baseline
  • Second failure: One-on-one coaching session, identify why they click
  • Third failure: Manager notification, mandatory additional training
  • Continued issues: Consider access restrictions, closer monitoring

Root Cause Analysis:

  • Is the training effective for their learning style?
  • Are they under time pressure that causes rushed decisions?
  • Do they understand the consequences?

The goal is behavior change, not punishment. People who feel shamed become disengaged from security entirely."

Q3 What metrics would you use to measure security awareness program effectiveness?
Sample Answer

"I'd track metrics across multiple dimensions to get a complete picture:

Phishing Simulation Metrics:

  • Click rate: % who clicked the phishing link (target: <5%)
  • Report rate: % who reported to IT/security (target: >70%)
  • Credential submission rate: % who entered credentials (target: <1%)
  • Time to report: How quickly are phishing emails reported?
  • Trend over time: Are metrics improving quarter-over-quarter?

Training Metrics:

  • Completion rate: % completing required training (target: 100%)
  • Quiz scores: Average assessment scores and pass rates
  • Time to complete: Are people rushing through or engaging?
  • Feedback scores: User satisfaction with training content

Behavioral Metrics:

  • DLP policy violations: Trending down indicates better data handling
  • Password reset requests: Fewer resets may indicate better password practices
  • Security incident reports: More reports = users are paying attention
  • Clean desk audit results: Physical security awareness

Reporting Approach:

  • Monthly dashboard for security team
  • Quarterly executive summary for leadership
  • Department-level breakdowns to identify high-risk groups
  • Year-over-year comparison to show program impact

The key is demonstrating ROI - when click rates drop from 20% to 5%, that's real risk reduction leadership can understand."

Q4 How would you handle a real phishing attack that bypassed your filters?
Sample Answer

"This is exactly why awareness training matters - it's our last line of defense. Here's my response plan:

Immediate Response (First 15 Minutes):

  • Confirm it's a real phishing attack, not a simulation
  • Use Exchange admin to search for all recipients of the email
  • Purge/quarantine unread copies from mailboxes
  • Block the sender and malicious URLs at the email gateway and web filter

User Communication (Within 1 Hour):

  • Send organization-wide alert: 'Phishing Alert - Do Not Click'
  • Include screenshot of the phishing email so people recognize it
  • Clear instructions: delete if received, report if clicked
  • Contact info for concerned users

Victim Triage:

  • Review email logs to identify who clicked
  • For clickers: Check if credentials were harvested, force password reset
  • For credential submitters: Immediate password reset, check for account compromise, review recent activity
  • Scan affected endpoints for malware if payload was involved

Post-Incident:

  • Report phishing domain to registrar for takedown
  • Update email filters with new IOCs
  • Consider targeted training for affected users
  • Document lessons learned

Speed is critical - every minute the phishing email sits in inboxes is another chance for someone to click."

Q5 What's the difference between security awareness and security training?
Sample Answer

"They're related but serve different purposes:

Security Awareness:

  • Goal: Change behavior and culture around security
  • Audience: All users, regardless of role
  • Content: Why security matters, what threats exist, how to recognize attacks
  • Format: Posters, newsletters, phishing simulations, lunch-and-learns
  • Outcome: Users think before they click, report suspicious activity

Security Training:

  • Goal: Build specific skills and knowledge
  • Audience: Role-based - different training for different roles
  • Content: How to use specific tools, procedures, compliance requirements
  • Format: Courses, certifications, hands-on labs, assessments
  • Outcome: Users know HOW to do security tasks correctly

Example Distinction:

  • Awareness: 'Phishing emails can steal your credentials' (why it matters)
  • Training: 'Here's how to report a phishing email using the Report Message button in Outlook' (how to do it)

A complete program needs both - awareness creates the motivation, training provides the skills."

Q6 How would you tailor security awareness for different user groups at a university?
Sample Answer

"Universities have diverse user populations with different needs and risk profiles:

Students:

  • Challenges: Short attention spans, BYOD, sharing accounts, financial aid scams
  • Approach: Short, mobile-friendly content; social media style; focus on personal security
  • Topics: Phishing, password managers, public WiFi dangers, identity theft
  • Delivery: Orientation, LMS modules, social media campaigns, peer ambassadors

Faculty:

  • Challenges: Research data protection, academic freedom culture, external collaboration
  • Approach: Respect their expertise; focus on protecting their research and reputation
  • Topics: Data classification, secure file sharing, conference travel security, research data handling
  • Delivery: Department meetings, email updates, one-on-one consultations for researchers

Administrative Staff:

  • Challenges: Handle sensitive data daily, targeted by social engineering, process-driven
  • Approach: Compliance-focused; clear procedures; emphasize data protection
  • Topics: PIPEDA compliance, data handling, incident reporting, business email compromise
  • Delivery: Mandatory annual training, role-specific modules, department-level sessions

IT Staff:

  • Challenges: Privileged access, targeted attacks, security fatigue
  • Approach: Advanced technical content; peer learning; stay current on threats
  • Topics: Privileged account security, incident response, emerging threats, secure development
  • Delivery: Technical workshops, threat briefings, CTF exercises, certifications

One-size-fits-all training doesn't work - people engage when content is relevant to their world."
