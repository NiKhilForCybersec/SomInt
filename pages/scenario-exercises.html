Scenario-Based Exercises

Practice responding to realistic technical scenarios you may encounter during your interview at IBU.

14.1 How to Approach Scenario Questions

Structured Problem-Solving Framework

SCENARIO RESPONSE FRAMEWORK

                    STRUCTURED PROBLEM-SOLVING APPROACH                      

                                                                             
      
     1. CLARIFY THE SITUATION                                              
          
     • Ask clarifying questions before diving in                           
     • Understand scope, constraints, and priorities                       
     • "Can I ask a few questions to make sure I understand?"              
      
                                                                            
                                                                            
      
     2. THINK OUT LOUD                                                     
          
     • Share your thought process - interviewers want to see HOW           
     • "My first thought is... but I should also consider..."              
     • Don't jump to solutions - show your analysis                        
      
                                                                            
                                                                            
      
     3. PRIORITIZE                                                         
          
     • For incidents: life safety → containment → eradication              
     • For projects: quick wins → high impact → foundation building        
     • State your prioritization logic explicitly                          
      
                                                                            
                                                                            
      
     4. PROPOSE ACTIONS                                                    
          
     • Specific, actionable steps with clear sequence                      
     • Include who, what, when where relevant                              
     • Address both immediate and long-term needs                          
      
                                                                            
                                                                            
      
     5. CONSIDER RISKS & ALTERNATIVES                                      
          
     • What could go wrong with your approach?                             
     • What alternatives exist?                                            
     • What tradeoffs are you making?
•
Pro Tip: It's Okay to Ask Questions
In scenario questions, interviewers often intentionally leave out details. Asking smart clarifying questions shows real-world problem-solving skills. Questions like "Is this during business hours?", "What's our backup situation?", or "Who else needs to be notified?" demonstrate experience.
Security Incident Scenarios

14.2 Security Incident Scenarios

S1 Ransomware Detection: Multiple endpoints show encryption activity at 2 AM.
Scenario Details

You're the on-call security engineer. At 2:15 AM, your phone alerts you to multiple high-severity detections from Defender for Endpoint. Five faculty office computers are showing signs of file encryption activity. The ransomware appears to be spreading.

Context: It's end of semester. Faculty are uploading final grades this week.

Model Response

Clarifying Questions I'd Ask:

  • Are the affected machines still online/connected to the network?
  • Do we have remote isolation capabilities?
  • When was the last known good backup of file shares?
  • Is the grading system cloud-based or on local servers?

Immediate Actions (First 15 minutes):

  • Isolate: Use Defender for Endpoint to isolate all affected machines immediately - this is the priority to stop lateral movement
  • Assess scope: Check if any file servers or the grading system shows activity
  • Preserve evidence: Start logging everything - timestamps, affected systems, alert details
  • Notification: Text IT Director that we have an active incident - they need to know even at 2 AM

Short-term Actions (Next 2 hours):

  • Identify patient zero - trace back through logs to find the entry point
  • Block IOCs at firewall and email gateway
  • Verify backup integrity before we need it
  • If spreading continues, consider isolating the entire faculty VLAN

Communication Plan:

  • Prepare an incident brief for leadership by 6 AM
  • Draft faculty communication (without causing panic) about temporary system unavailability
  • Coordinate with Registrar about grading deadline implications

Why This Approach:

Containment is the absolute priority with ransomware - every minute of spread means more data at risk. The timing near grade submission is critical context, so I'd ensure the grading system is protected/isolated. Communication is key because faculty will notice when their computers are isolated.

S2 Phishing Campaign: Students receiving fake financial aid emails.
Scenario Details

The Financial Aid office calls you: students are reporting emails claiming to be from the university, asking them to "verify their banking information" to receive their tuition refund. Multiple students have already clicked the link. It's the start of the semester when refunds are actually being processed.

Model Response

Clarifying Questions:

  • How many students have reported this so far?
  • Did anyone actually enter their banking information?
  • Can I see a copy of the email and the link?

Immediate Technical Actions:

  • Block the malicious URL at our web filter/firewall immediately
  • Search email logs to identify all recipients of the phishing email
  • Quarantine remaining unread copies in student mailboxes using Exchange admin tools
  • Add sender to blocklist if external, or investigate if internal account compromise

Victim Triage:

  • For students who clicked: Check for credential submission, scan their devices if possible
  • For students who entered banking info: Urgent outreach - they need to contact their bank immediately
  • Force password reset for anyone who entered credentials on the phishing page

Communication (Critical):

  • Coordinate with Financial Aid to send official "this is a scam" notice immediately
  • Use multiple channels: email, student portal alert, social media if available
  • Include clear guidance: "We will never ask for banking information via email"
  • Provide contact info for students who may have been compromised

Follow-up:

  • Report the phishing domain for takedown
  • Document for potential reporting to law enforcement if students suffered financial loss
  • Consider targeted security awareness for students around financial aid season
S3 Data Breach: Student records found on dark web forum.
Scenario Details

Your threat intelligence feed alerts you that a dataset claiming to be from your university is being sold on a dark web forum. The sample includes what appears to be student names, email addresses, student IDs, and program enrollment information for approximately 2,000 students.

Model Response

Clarifying Questions:

  • Can we verify this is actually our data vs. fabricated?
  • What's the date range of the student records?
  • Does it include any financial information (SIN, banking)?

Verification Phase:

  • Validate the data: Cross-reference sample records with our student database to confirm authenticity
  • Assess the scope: What data fields are included? Is this a partial or complete dump?
  • Timeline: Based on the data, can we estimate when the breach occurred?

Immediate Escalation:

  • This is a reportable incident under PIPEDA - escalate to leadership and legal immediately
  • Engage our incident response plan for data breaches
  • Consider engaging external forensics if we can't determine the source

Investigation:

  • Audit access logs for the student information system
  • Check for any unusual data exports or queries
  • Review any third-party integrations that have access to student data
  • Look for compromised accounts with access to this data

Regulatory Requirements (PIPEDA):

  • Report to Privacy Commissioner if there's "real risk of significant harm"
  • Notify affected individuals as soon as feasible
  • Keep records of the breach and our response

What I Would NOT Do:

  • Attempt to contact or engage with the threat actor
  • Delay notification to "wait and see"
  • Assume it's fake without verification
Infrastructure Scenarios

14.3 Infrastructure & Operations Scenarios

S4 Network Outage: Campus WiFi goes down during peak hours.
Scenario Details

It's 10 AM on a Tuesday - peak class time. The help desk is being flooded with calls that WiFi isn't working across campus. Students can't access online exams, faculty can't use their presentation materials, and administrators can't access cloud applications. The Meraki dashboard is showing multiple access points offline.

Model Response

Clarifying Questions:

  • Is this affecting all buildings or specific areas?
  • Are wired connections still working?
  • Did anything change recently (firmware update, configuration change)?

Immediate Triage (First 5 minutes):

  • Check Meraki Dashboard: What's the status showing? How many APs are offline vs. online?
  • Pattern recognition: Is it random APs or entire buildings/switches?
  • Check upstream: Is internet connectivity working? Can we reach Meraki cloud?

Common Causes to Check:

  • DHCP exhaustion: Check DHCP scope - are we out of addresses?
  • Licensing: Meraki requires active license - did it expire?
  • Core switch issue: If all APs are offline, check the core network infrastructure
  • ISP outage: If dashboard is unreachable, could be upstream issue

Communication:

  • Acknowledge to help desk: "We're aware and investigating"
  • Post status update to internal channels
  • If exams are affected: Notify Registrar/Faculty Affairs immediately for contingency

Escalation Path:

  • If ISP issue: Contact provider immediately with business impact
  • If hardware failure: Meraki support (or vendor depending on support contract)
  • If cause unknown after 15 minutes: Escalate to IT Director

Post-Incident:

  • Root cause analysis
  • Consider monitoring improvements to detect earlier
  • Review backup connectivity options for critical functions like exams
S5 New Employee Onboarding: Design the Day 1 IT experience.
Scenario Details

IBU is hiring 15 new staff members starting next month, including faculty, administrative staff, and IT support. Currently, new employee IT setup is inconsistent and takes 2-3 days. Leadership wants Day 1 to be productive. Design the process.

Model Response

Clarifying Questions:

  • What's the current lead time between HR confirming hire and start date?
  • Do we have standard hardware ready or is it ordered per person?
  • What role-based access templates exist today?

Pre-Day 1 Process (HR triggers IT):

  • T-5 days: HR submits new hire ticket with: name, role, department, manager, start date
  • T-4 days: Create AD account, email, assign to appropriate security groups based on role template
  • T-3 days: Provision M365 licenses, Teams membership, department SharePoint access
  • T-2 days: Configure device (laptop/desktop), install standard software, enroll in Intune
  • T-1 day: Stage equipment at employee's desk or reception, prepare welcome packet

Day 1 Experience:

  • Morning: Equipment waiting at desk, pre-printed credentials in sealed envelope
  • First login: Guided MFA setup (Authenticator app), password change
  • Welcome email auto-sent: Links to IT resources, help desk info, key policies
  • Security awareness: Assign initial training module in LMS

Role-Based Templates:

Role TypeStandard AccessSpecial Considerations
FacultyLMS admin, course sites, research drivesMay need specialized software
Admin StaffSharePoint, department shares, SIS readFinancial system access via manager request
IT StaffAdmin tools, elevated permissionsSeparate admin accounts, PAM access

Success Metrics:

  • 100% of employees productive within 2 hours of arrival
  • Zero Day 1 help desk tickets for account issues
  • All security training assigned within 24 hours

14.4 Strategic Planning Scenarios

S6 Budget Prioritization: You have $50K for security improvements. What do you do?
Scenario Details

You've been given $50,000 in budget for security improvements at IBU. The university currently has M365 E3 licensing, Cisco Meraki network, and basic endpoint protection. What would you prioritize?

Model Response

Clarifying Questions:

  • What's the biggest pain point or risk today?
  • Any recent incidents that highlighted gaps?
  • Is this one-time money or ongoing annual budget?
  • Are there any compliance deadlines driving priorities?

My Recommended Approach:

I'd focus on maximizing what we already own before buying new tools:

Option A: Maximize M365 (if E3 → E5 is possible)

  • Upgrade to M365 E5 Security add-on (~$12/user/month)
  • Unlocks: Defender for Endpoint P2, Defender for Identity, Cloud App Security, Advanced eDiscovery
  • For 100 users: ~$15,000/year - huge capability gain

Option B: If staying with E3, invest in:

InvestmentEst. CostRisk Addressed
Backup solution (Veeam/Datto)$15-20KRansomware recovery
Security awareness training (KnowBe4)$5-8KPhishing risk
Vulnerability scanner (Nessus)$3-5KUnknown vulnerabilities
PAM solution (basic)$10-15KAdmin credential theft
Incident response retainer$5-10KExpert help when needed

My Top 3 Priorities:

  1. Backup & Recovery ($20K): Non-negotiable foundation - everything else fails without reliable backups
  2. Security Awareness ($7K): Biggest ROI per dollar - reduces phishing success significantly
  3. Remaining $23K: Either E5 upgrade or vulnerability scanning + PAM depending on current maturity

What I Would NOT Do:

  • Buy a SIEM with this budget - too expensive to operate properly
  • Invest in advanced tools before basics are solid
  • Spend it all on hardware with ongoing maintenance costs
S7 Policy Creation: Develop a BYOD policy for students and faculty.
Scenario Details

IBU wants to allow students and faculty to access university resources from personal devices. Currently there's no formal BYOD policy. What elements would you include and how would you enforce it?

Model Response

Policy Framework:

1. Scope & Definitions:

  • Define "personal device" (phones, tablets, laptops)
  • Define "university resources" (email, LMS, SharePoint, WiFi)
  • Specify who is covered (students, faculty, staff)

2. Minimum Security Requirements:

  • Device must have passcode/biometric lock enabled
  • Operating system must be within 2 versions of current (no XP, no Android 8)
  • Device must not be jailbroken/rooted
  • Encryption must be enabled (default on modern devices)

3. Technical Enforcement (Intune MAM):

  • Use App Protection Policies - containerize university data without managing entire device
  • Require PIN to access Outlook/Teams/OneDrive apps
  • Block copy/paste from work apps to personal apps
  • Remote wipe capability for university data only (not personal photos)

4. Network Segmentation:

  • BYOD devices connect to separate SSID
  • Limited access to internet and cloud services only
  • No direct access to internal servers or sensitive systems

5. User Responsibilities:

  • Report lost/stolen devices immediately
  • Accept that university can wipe university data remotely
  • Keep device updated and malware-free
  • Not attempt to circumvent security controls

6. Privacy Protections (Important for buy-in):

  • IT cannot see personal apps, photos, messages, or browsing history
  • Only university app data is visible and manageable
  • Personal device location is not tracked

Implementation Approach:

  • Start with optional enrollment - incentivize with better WiFi access
  • Provide clear documentation and FAQ
  • Phase in requirements over time rather than hard cutoff
